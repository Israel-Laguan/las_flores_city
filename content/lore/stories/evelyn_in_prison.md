# Evelyn Ruthenberg: Prison and Trial (2077–2081)

## Overview

Evelyn Ruthenberg's trial was not just a legal proceeding—it was the culmination of her twenty-year campaign and the city's reckoning with its own corruption. Her imprisonment, though physically confining, became another battlefield in her war for truth.

---

## The Arrest (May 2077)

When Adeyemi emerged from Mayor Vega's estate with Evelyn in handcuffs, the world saw a vigilante captured. What they didn't see was the quiet understanding between them—the shared knowledge that the story being presented was necessary, but not entirely true.

Evelyn was taken to the Central Detention Facility. The cell was clean by prison standards—she had, after all, just helped expose the corruption that ran the city. But it was still a cell.

The first night, she slept for fourteen hours. Not the restless sleep of someone hunted, but the deep, exhausted sleep of someone who had finally stopped running.

---

## The Trial (June–December 2077)

### The Prosecution's Case

Prosecutor Hendrikson had an airtight case on the facts: Evelyn had killed Mayor Alfonso Vega and eight other individuals over two decades. The evidence was forensic, documented, irrefutable.

What he didn't have was motive. Not the kind that would satisfy a jury that wanted to understand *why*.

### The Defense Strategy

Evelyn's defense team, led by a young public defender named Elena Vasquez, could have argued insanity. They could have argued self-defense. They could have tried to paint Evelyn as a victim of circumstance.

Evelyn refused all of these approaches.

Her instruction to her lawyers was simple: *"I am guilty of what I did. But I am not the only one."*

### Evelyn Takes the Stand

For three days, Evelyn Ruthenberg testified. She did not weep. She did not rage. She spoke with the same cold precision she had applied to her vigilante campaign.

She laid out the complete history:

- The Great Lithium Leak and the cover-up
- Karla and Nubia's murders
- The suppressed Carinata evidence
- The rigged 2057 trial
- Yara Rossi's assassination
- The Retirement Complex Murders and why she did them

She produced documents that had not been part of the estate broadcast—deeper financial records, internal communications, the full chain of command.

She named names. Not just the dead, but the living. Officials who had accepted bribes. Judges who had dismissed evidence. Journalists who had buried stories.

And then she said the words that would become the most quoted of the trial:

*"I am guilty of what I did. But I am not the only one. The question is whether this city is ready to hold everyone accountable, or only the ones who fought back."*

### The Verdict

The jury deliberated for twelve days. In the end, they convicted Evelyn on reduced charges—manslaughter rather than murder—reflecting their recognition that while her actions were illegal, they were not the acts of a common criminal.

She was sentenced to fifteen years in a minimum-security facility.

---

## Prison Life (2077–2081)

### The First Year: Isolation

Evelyn requested solitary confinement. Not as punishment, but as protection—for herself and for others. Two decades of operating alone had made her used to silence.

She spent her days reading. The prison library had a surprising selection, and she worked her way through history, philosophy, and—when she allowed herself—fiction.

She wrote letters. To Miguel, Isabella, Ana. To the families of her victims. Not asking for forgiveness, but acknowledging the pain she had caused.

She did not write to Adeyemi. They both knew some conversations couldn't happen on paper.

### The Routine

By year two, Evelyn had established a routine:

- **Morning**: Exercise in the yard. She had lost weight in prison.
- **Afternoon**: Teaching basic engineering to other inmates. The warden allowed it; it kept the peace.
- **Evening**: Reading, or listening to the radio. The news was full of the city's reconstruction.

She followed the rebuilding of Las Flores with a scholar's detachment. She had done her part. Now it was someone else's turn.

### The Visit That Changed Everything

In late 2078, Miguel visited. He brought news: the transitional council had been formed. Alex's movement was becoming the new government. The city was healing.

And then he said the words that broke her composure:

*"They named the bench after him. 'This neighborhood will be better built next time.'"*

For the first time in prison, Evelyn Ruthenberg wept.

---

## The Commutation (2081)

In early 2081, the transitional government announced a broad amnesty for non-violent political prisoners. Evelyn's case was... complicated.

But her cooperation with the international forensic investigation, her testimony, her years of evidence gathering—these counted for something.

On March 15, 2081, after serving four years of her fifteen-year sentence, Evelyn Ruthenberg walked out of the Central Detention Facility.

She was met by Miguel. He asked if she wanted to go to Sector Norte, to see the bench.

She said she would. But not yet.

---

## Themes

**The cost of justice.** Evelyn's trial was not just about her crimes—it was about the city's crimes. The jury's reduced sentence acknowledged that some laws are written to protect the guilty.

**Prison as another kind of shadow.** Evelyn had spent twenty years in the shadows, fighting a war no one else could see. Prison was just another kind of shadow—one where she could finally rest.

**The difficulty of re-entry.** The city Evelyn returned to was not the city she had left. She had spent two decades fighting to change it. Now she had to learn how to live in it.
