## Overview

The story of Residencial Las Estrellas is, at its core, a story about what people do with blood money. The retired executives and municipal officials who co-purchased the complex were not monsters by the standards of their world. They were networked, practical people who had been given a choiceŌĆösign the documents or miss the opportunityŌĆöand had signed. That the documents concealed environmental cover-ups, that the opportunity was funded by contamination that killed hundreds, that the land beneath the building had never been properly remediated: these were facts they preferred to keep at a comfortable distance.

The Retirement Complex Murders is Evelyn Ruthenberg's accounting.

## Background: The Architects of the Complex

In 2062, a group of fourteen individuals pooled their resources to purchase and develop Residencial Las Estrellas. The buyers were drawn from two overlapping circles:

- **Former Minera Estrella middle management:** Engineers, project directors, and environmental compliance officers who had been instrumental in the 2052 Lithium Leak cover-up and had profited from subsequent regulatory manipulation.
- **City Council staff and municipal officers:** Inspectors, permit administrators, and environmental liaisons who had looked the other way at the right moments, approved the right reports, and accepted the right payments.

The land itself had been sold to them at a fraction of its market value by Minera EstrellaŌĆöa company that no longer needed the parcel now that the litigation risk had passed and the political climate had shifted in their favor. The sale was a reward. It was also a final link in the chain of complicity. Everyone who signed the purchase deed was now bound to the others by shared legal exposure.

Most had retired comfortably by the late 2070s. Some had family. Some had grandchildren they photographed on holidays. They lived quietly in the complex they had built, in a neighborhood that was pleasant if unglamorous, and they did not think much about the river downstream or the communities it had passed through.

Evelyn Ruthenberg thought about nothing else.

## The Campaign: How Evelyn Operated

Evelyn was not trained for what she became. She was a former process engineerŌĆösomeone who understood material flows, pressure systems, and the invisible logic of infrastructure. What she became, over two decades of isolation and rage, was a specialist in the application of that knowledge to human ends.

She killed no one with her hands. She created conditions.

**The HVAC Method:** The building's aging climate control system was centrally managed but locally modifiable. By accessing the rooftop control unitsŌĆöwhich required nothing more than a utility knife, specific electrical knowledge, and timeŌĆöshe could disable ventilation in a targeted unit while maintaining the building's overall pressure readings. Carbon monoxide accumulated slowly, invisibly, in the sleep-hours of people who had taken so much from others and now had only this quiet apartment and this quiet life.

**Vehicle Tampering:** For targets who lived more mobile lives or who seemed alert to unusual events in the building, Evelyn used brake line manipulation. The method required access to the vehicleŌĆötypically accomplished through the building's shared parking structureŌĆöand a precision that came from studying vehicle systems for months before acting.

**Electrical Interference:** For certain targets, she engineered electrical faults that appeared accidental: a short in an appliance, an overloaded circuit, a building-system anomaly that investigators with limited forensic resources would close as infrastructure failure.

She worked slowly. Years between some deaths. She was in no hurry. She was making a complete accounting, not a massacre.

By 2077, eight of the fourteen original owners of the complex were dead. Four had died of natural causesŌĆöshe felt neither satisfaction nor disappointment about this. Two had moved away and were lower on her list. The remaining four in the complex were her current targets.

## Alex Moves In

Alex Garcia arrived at Residencial Las Estrellas in early 2077 looking for cheap housing near the university train line. They were given the studio on the fourth floor. They propped sketchbooks on the windowsill and called it fine.

Within weeks, two residents were dead.

Alex did not immediately connect the deaths to malice. The building was old. The residents were elderly. But Alex had studied the thermodynamic logic of HVAC systems as part of a campus sustainability module, and the combination of factorsŌĆöthe timing of the deaths relative to the apparent system failures, the age and design of the rooftop units, the fact that the apartments in question were both on the northeast corner of the building where the main duct trunk ranŌĆöbothered them in a way they could not articulate and could not dismiss.

The night they heard footsteps above and went up to the roof changed the direction of everything.

## The Amateur Investigation

### Week One: Roof and Razor

Alex found the utility knife on the rooftop and photographed it before touching it. They were, as they would later admit, already thinking like someone who intended to present evidence. They also found residue where the electrical apparatus had been connected and goneŌĆöscuff marks, a small patch of oil from the insulation compound used on the wiring. They documented it all.

Hidden behind a vent, Alex discovered a crumpled piece of paper bearing a cryptic sequence of characters ŌĆö possibly a code or coordinates. Before they could examine it further, footsteps approached. Miguel appeared, looking disheveled, asking what Alex was doing on the roof. Alex discreetly stowed the paper away, mentioning only that they were "investigating." The paper's meaning would remain unclear for weeks.

When Chief Inspector Adeyemi arrived and conducted his initial interview, Alex had the strong and uncomfortable sense that the inspector's attentiveness was diagnostic rather than cooperative. Adeyemi was listening to figure out whether Alex was a witness or a suspect or something in between. He advised them to call immediately if they saw anything else, and Alex understood this as a conditional tolerance: *you can keep watching, but keep it within limits I can manage.*

Alex, being Alex, kept watching.

### Week Two: The Apparatus Disappears

The electrical apparatus appeared on the roof three days after Adeyemi's visit. Alex photographed it. They contacted Adeyemi. By the time the inspector arrivedŌĆötwo hours laterŌĆöit was gone. The only remnant was a distinctive oil smear on the HVAC housing.

Another resident was dead by the following morning.

Adeyemi was visibly angry. Not at AlexŌĆöat the apparatus's disappearance, at the gap in his ability to act on incomplete evidence. This was one of the earliest signs that the inspector was operating inside constraints he had not chosen and did not fully endorse. He was not protecting the killer. He was navigating a system that had been engineered to make effective investigation difficult, and he had learnedŌĆöover a long and costly careerŌĆöthe difference between the fights worth having and the ones that ended badly.

He wasn't ready yet to fight this one.

### Week Three: Carlos and the Camera

Miguel confirmed Alex's suspicion that this was beyond what one person should handle alone, andŌĆöcharacteristicallyŌĆöbegan solving the logistical problem before the conversation was finished. He knew **Carlos**, an electrical engineering student. Carlos was the kind of person who said yes before the favor was fully explained, who showed up twenty minutes early, who made difficult technical things sound like puzzles worth solving.

Carlos confirmed within two visits to the rooftop unit that the HVAC system could absolutely be hijacked. His analysis was precise: whoever had done this "bypassed the safety protocols" and "added a secondary system to introduce carbon monoxide directly into the air ducts." He deemed it sophisticated and premeditated.

They installed a hidden camera on the rooftop control housing. The setup took an evening and two visits to an electronics supply store in the university district. Carlos wired it to a passive recorder that could be retrieved without accessing the roof.

Three nights later, the camera caught it: a figure moving carefully along the roofline in the early morning hours, accessing the HVAC housing, working with the focused, unhurried precision of someone who had done this before. The figure's face was not capturedŌĆöthey worked with their back to the camera angleŌĆöbut the gasoline-powered vehicle waiting in the loading zone below was visible.

### Week Four: The Vacant Room and Shifting Vantage

The camera was discovered the following day ŌĆö not by the killer, but by the building's maintenance crew, who reported it to management. The camera was removed. The team's first lead was gone.

But the day after the camera was removed, Alex noticed a shadow flitting through the hallway late at night. They alerted Miguel and Carlos. The killer was still active, still watching.

Carlos provided a second camera. The team targeted a room adjacent to the apartments of the remaining building owners ŌĆö a supposedly vacant unit. The setup was more discreet this time, positioned to capture the corridor's comings and goings.

Their patience paid off. The camera captured a figure stealthily entering the vacant room through a window ŌĆö a move that spoke of careful planning and knowledge of the building's layout. When the team rushed to the room, they found it deserted, but Miguel's sharp eye caught signs of recent presence: a suspiciously clean area near the window, possibly used for surveillance. The killer had been using the vacant room as a vantage point.

With the camera now known to the killer, they shifted their vantage point to one of four possible windows facing the building. The team now faced a choice: which window to watch? They chose to take matters into their own hands, beginning a stakeout that would last for days.

### The Old Gas Car and the Age Pattern

The stakeout paid off when the team spotted an **old gasoline-powered car** ŌĆö conspicuous in a city that had largely converted to electric and self-driving vehicles ŌĆö parked near the building. The figure from the roof was seen entering this vehicle. It became the team's primary target.

Miguel, reviewing the case notes, noticed a pattern: all the victims were approximately **60 years old**. This age range corresponded to the cohort of mid-level managers and municipal officers who had been in positions of power during the 2052 disaster and its cover-up.

### The Chase and Carlos's Death

The pursuit of the vehicle was impulsive and catastrophic. Alex saw it parked near the building one afternoon and acted before anyone had agreed on a plan. Miguel arrived. Carlos arrived. Ana, who happened to be meeting Alex that day, arrived and said she wasn't going anywhere and would help.

The car fled south. They followed in two vehicles. On the road to the outer districts, a motorcycle appeared and cut across Carlos's lane. It was not random.

Carlos died in the crash. The motorcycle continued south and was never traced.

There is a moment, in every investigation into injustice, when it stops being abstract. When someone you know is the cost. The four students who met at a caf├® afterwardŌĆöAlex, Miguel, Ana, Isabella, who had arrived at the caf├® independently and stayed when she understood what had happenedŌĆöwere different people than they had been that morning.

## Uncovering the Network

Isabella brought discipline to what had been instinct. She created a cross-referenced database of the building's residents, all documented deaths in the complex going back five years, and every publicly available record connecting the victims to shared affiliations.

But she went further than the building. Cross-checking against national death registries and archived municipal incident reports, she found a pattern that extended beyond Las Flores: similar carbon monoxide poisoning deaths in three other cities over the preceding eight years, all involving elderly victims living alone, all attributed to infrastructure failure. No investigation had ever connected them. No one had looked. Isabella connected them in four days.

The implication was significant: the killer had been operating for longer than the Las Estrellas murders suggested, and had refined the method across multiple jurisdictions before arriving here. This was not improvised. It was a campaign.

### The Photograph and the Twist

The photograph was the breakthrough. Found in a city archive database that required three access workarounds to reach: the 2058 inauguration of the R├Ło de las Flores lithium filtration facility. Fourteen people smiling in front of a new structure. Ana identified four of them as former public-relations staff from Minera EstrellaŌĆöfound through an archived newspaper article she retrieved using the university library's press database, the same system she navigated every day at work. Isabella matched two more to city environmental department records. The remaining eight were traced through business registration records to the ownership consortium of Residencial Las Estrellas.

Every victim was in that photograph. The building had been purchased by the people in that photograph.

There was a disorienting interim before the full picture resolved. Initially the team read the victims' public record as that of whistle-blowers and critics: several had given public testimony against Luz del R├Ło; one had appeared at a municipal hearing arguing for stricter environmental enforcement. The team's early working theory was that the killer was targeting people who had *exposed* the mining companyŌĆöthat the motive was corporate retaliation.

The City Hall records inverted everything. The men and women who had made public noise about environmental standards had done so while privately accepting the land deal, suppressing internal reports, and profiting from the same contamination they performed outrage about. Their public criticism was cover, or worseŌĆöa way of controlling the narrative from inside the corruption. They weren't victims of the system. They were architects of it.

The moment Alex understood this, the moral geometry of the whole investigation shifted. They were not hunting a monster who targeted heroes. They were hunting someone who had spent years doing what Alex could not quite name as wrong.

One of the three surviving building ownersŌĆöan elderly retired assistant comptroller who was still living in the complexŌĆöwas approached by Isabella and asked about the others. He looked at the list of names and said only: *Maybe it was karma. That man was corrupt. He used to be a traitor.*

He meant the engineer who had died in the mall crash. That engineer had *testified against Luz del R├Ło*ŌĆöhe had broken from his circle and given evidence that helped expose the company. To his former colleagues, this made him a traitor twice over: to the mining company, and to their shared secret. He had both profited from the corruption and helped bring one of its instruments to account. The "karma" the surviving owner cited was not abstract. It was specific, personal, and self-incriminating. It was the first moment the team understood that even the victims knew exactly what they were.

### The City Hall Break-In

The night Isabella and Miguel broke into the City Hall archives, it was Isabella's plan from the start. She had grown impatient with passive camera surveillanceŌĆöthe waiting felt like watching a clock run down. She did not wait for things to come to her.

While Miguel physically contained one guard with the blunt competence of someone who had grown up doing hard physical work and had been in enough confrontations not to panic in one, and Isabella knocked out a second guard who had Ana pinned, Isabella was already at the records room door, photographing what she needed, and gone before the guards understood she had been there.

It was tactically correct. To Ana, even knowing this, it felt like abandonment. This distinction did not fully resolve between them.

Ana walked out with a swollen eye, bruised ribs, and a wrist that would ache for months. In the car on the way back, while Isabella was already talking about what the documents confirmed, Ana waited for a full stop and said: *I'm done. Not because I don't believe this. ## Adeyemi's Maneuver

When he announced the arrest of Diego Reyes and declared the case closed, Alex's first instinct was relief. Their second instinctŌĆöalmost immediateŌĆöwas wrongness.

Adeyemi had arrested a man with a real grievance, evidence that looked airtight, and a story the city wanted to believe was over. The case evaporated like steam.

Two days later, two more building owners were dead of cardiac events. Within a forty-eight hour window. Both in the same building. Both with the same condition listed on their death certificates.

Adeyemi had not arrested the killer. He had closed the paperwork around someone convenient and gone back to managing the situation. ## The Confrontation

Alex and Isabella found Sandoval in a rented room near the port. He was an unremarkable man who had done unremarkable evil and was now, at the end of his life, terrified in a way that had not been available to him when the decisions were made. He didn't understand why he had been targeted more than the others. He kept saying he had only done what they all did.

They staked the room for three days. On the third night, Evelyn Ruthenberg appeared in the courtyard below.

What Alex had expectedŌĆöthe confrontation with a deranged killer, the opportunity to prove something, the clean satisfaction of being rightŌĆöwas not what happened. What happened was a stairwell, a woman older than expected, and a silence that lasted long enough for Alex to understand they were in the presence of someone who had already decided whether they were a threat.

Evelyn showed Alex the documents. She did not explain or defend. She presented evidence the way you present a completed account: here is what was done, here is who did it, here is what was lost.

When Evelyn showed Alex the health survey that listed their hometown as a contaminated downstream community, the conversation changed register.

Alex came out of the stairwell forty minutes later. Isabella, who had waited outside with her hand near the emergency call function on her phone, says Alex looked like someone who had just been told a truth they had already suspected for a long time.

## Resolution and Transition

Diego Reyes, the falsely arrested man, was eventually released when the final broadcast of 2077 made the true nature of the case impossible to suppress. His legal process was one of dozens set in motion in the weeks after the data became public.

Aurelio Sandoval survived. His testimonyŌĆögiven to both the provisional council and the international forensic team that reviewed the Las Flores corruption casesŌĆöprovided the final chain of documentary evidence connecting the original fourteen building owners to the Minera Estrella cover-up, the judicial manipulation of the 2057 trial, and the subsequent LW Group consolidation of the city's political infrastructure.

He did not ask for mercy in exchange. He said he just wanted to finish telling it correctly, since the first timeŌĆötwenty years ago, when he had signed the documents and made himself complicitŌĆöhe had told it wrong.

## Themes

**Complicity as infrastructure.** The building owners were not individually powerful. Their power came from their networked silence. Each person who signed a document made it easier for the next person to sign. The system was not maintained by monsters but by people who made practical choices and accumulated enough to retire on.

**Justice delayed and corrupted.** The official investigation into the murders was not incompetent. It was managed. Adeyemi was not a fool. He was a man operating inside constraints he had accepted over a long career, and the murders at the complex represented a case where those constraints served the wrong people. His eventual choice to refuse Mayor Vega's execution order was not a sudden awakeningŌĆöit was the culmination of a crisis of conscience that the complexity of the case had forced to the surface.

**What amateurs can do.** Alex, Miguel, Carlos, Ana, and Isabella cracked a twenty-year cover-up without resources, without training, and without the protection of any institution. They also paid for it: Carlos with his life, Ana with injury and permanent exit, all of them with a kind of innocence that does not come back.
