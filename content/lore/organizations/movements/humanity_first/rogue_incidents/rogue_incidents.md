# Humanity First — Rogue Incidents

> Tags: `#humanity_first` `#conflict` `#accountability`

A documented record of individuals and groups who infiltrated or misused Humanity First, and how the organization responded. These incidents shaped HF's accountability systems and serve as cautionary tales.

---

## 1. Luis "El Tigre" Peralta — Community Security Initiative (2037–2038)

**Background:** Charismatic ex-policeman who proposed a "Community Security Initiative" for impoverished neighborhoods.

### What Went Wrong
- **Militaristic Tactics:** Night patrols and "stop-and-check" procedures — invasive and reminiscent of oppressive policing
- **Intimidation & Abuse:** Targeted vulnerable individuals; community members felt unsafe
- **Ideological Conflict:** "People only respect authority if they fear it" — completely at odds with HF values

### Outcome
- **Banished** (February 2038) after internal investigation revealed harassment and abuse
- Group disbanded; council publicly distanced from his actions
- Led to stricter screening for alignment with HF principles

---

## 2. Diego Salazar & Carla Ruiz — The Renovation Fraud (2038–2040)

**Background:** Ambitious entrepreneur and charismatic organizer who joined HF for influence and access to donor funds.

### What Went Wrong
- **Misappropriated Funds:** Diverted grant money to personal expenses
- **Staged Progress:** Photographed partially completed cleanups as proof of work
- **Deflection:** Accused the council of "stifling innovation" when questioned
- **Social Media Campaign:** Portrayed themselves as victims of "bureaucratic interference"

### Timeline
- Dec 2038 — Begin building following within HF
- Feb 2039 — First initiative (renovation fund); funds diverted
- Jun 2039 — Council raises concerns; Diego deflects
- Oct 2039 — Public accountability meeting; vague reports
- Feb 2040 — Internal audit; funding suspended
- Jul 2040 — Diego & Carla launch social media deflection campaign
- Dec 2040 — Audit findings made public; both expelled

### Outcome
- Photographic evidence of fraud published
- Community sentiment turned against them
- Council implemented prevention measures to stop future misuse

---

## 3. The Prosperidad Collective — Economic Exploitation (2041–2043)

**Background:** Group of middle-aged business owners led by **Mario Gutierrez** who saw HF as a vehicle for promoting their own ventures.

### What Went Wrong
- **Personal Profit over Community:** Proposed "community-owned" initiatives structured to generate revenue for their private companies
- **Exploitative Workshops:** Overcharged participants for superficial "business workshops"; kept profits
- **Anti-Cooperative Ideology:** Promoted individualistic, profit-driven mentality ("Be your own boss") that contradicted HF's cooperative ethos

### Outcome
- **Expelled** (mid-2043) after complaints and financial audit
- Mario Gutierrez publicly denounced
- Council issued refunds to workshop participants
- Strengthened financial oversight protocols

---

## 4. Valentina Rojas — "Spiritual Awakening" Movement (2045–2046)

**Background:** Local spiritual leader who gained followers through charisma and alternative healing practices.

### What Went Wrong
- **Fringe Beliefs:** Integrated esoteric practices — "energy alignment" and "spiritual cleansing" — into HF activities
- **Anti-Science:** Encouraged members to abstain from modern medicine
- **Exploitation:** Charged fees for "healing sessions"; pressured vulnerable members into "sacrificial donations"
- **Sectarian Behavior:** Attempted to position herself as essential guide to HF's "true path"

### Outcome
- **Removed** (early 2046) after investigation found exploitative practices
- Council held workshops on evidence-based practices and community support boundaries
- HF clarified guidelines; prohibited monetized spiritual services within the movement

---

## 5. The Red Sun Collective — Radical Environmentalism (2052–2055)

**Background:** Young, passionate activists who emerged after the Great Lithium Leakage, believing peaceful protests were ineffective.

### What Went Wrong
- **Promoted Violence:** Openly advocated sabotage — vandalism, disabling mining equipment, blocking access roads
- **Damaged HF's Image:** Negative media painted HF as extremist; authorities used Red Sun as excuse to scrutinize HF
- **Internal Defiance:** Called the council "weak" and claimed they had "sold out"; refused to back down when confronted

### Outcome
- **Expelled** (2055) after series of clashes
- Council held public meeting reaffirming non-violence and responsible activism
- Brief decline in public support due to media scrutiny
- Trust regained through continued transparency and peaceful action

---

## 6. Tomas Herrera — Infrastructure Development Scandal (2057–2058)

**Background:** Well-connected contractor with claimed experience in sustainable architecture who offered to manage eco-friendly infrastructure projects.

### What Went Wrong
- **Fund Misappropriation:** Inflated project costs; skimmed funds for personal use; provided vague invoices
- **Nepotism:** Hired unqualified relatives and friends; poor project management
- **Substandard Work:** Structures built were low quality; fell into disrepair shortly after completion
- **Reputation Damage:** Scandal became public in late 2058

### Outcome
- **Banned** from HF
- Full audit of all finances conducted
- **Stricter protocols:** Third-party audits mandatory for large projects
- Temporary reputation hit, but council's swift action rebuilt credibility

---

## Lessons & Reforms

Each incident led to progressively stronger accountability systems:

| Incident | Reform |
|---|---|
| El Tigre (2037) | Screening new members for principle alignment |
| Diego & Carla (2040) | Photographic/video documentation required for projects |
| Prosperidad (2043) | Financial oversight; prohibition of monetized private services |
| Valentina (2046) | Guidelines on acceptable practices; no monetized spiritual services |
| Red Sun (2055) | Public reaffirmation of non-violence; media transparency |
| Tomas (2058) | Third-party audits for large projects; anti-nepotism rules |

These incidents — and HF's transparent handling of them — ultimately **strengthened** the movement's credibility and resilience.
