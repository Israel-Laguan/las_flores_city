# Centro Cultural Chino-Latino

> Tags: `#landmark` `#central-city` `#culture`

## Overview

The Centro Cultural Chino-Latino was inaugurated in 2060 — two years after the industrial riots that had convulsed the city's working-class districts and left the three communities most entangled in the violence struggling to share territory without a shared institutional space. LW Group's social-affairs division proposed the Cultural Centre as a peace gesture — a shared civic building that would house programming from all three communities under one roof, funded through a five-year corporate grant that was renewed in 2065 and 2070.

Critics from all three communities accepted the building while rejecting the framing: a corporation that had materially benefited from the conditions producing the riots offering a cultural venue as settlement was not, in their reading, a peace gesture but a deflection. The grant terms included a booking-veto clause for programming "inconsistent with community harmony objectives."

## Notable Places

### Main Hall
Four-hundred-capacity event space with adaptive holographic décor configurable per community booking. The most contested resource on the joint management board's calendar.

### Shared Community Café
A central café in the common area intended as the building's primary integration space. In practice, tables self-organise by language affinity by midmorning.

### Multilingual Reading Room
A library space with materials in Spanish, Cantonese, Mandarin, and Dutch, supplemented by holographic text interfaces.

### Holographic Community History Corridor
A connecting corridor displaying rotating holographic narratives from the Chinese, Latino, and Afro-Dutch communities.

## Conclusion

The Centro Cultural Chino-Latino is the city's most honest institutional embodiment of its three-community tension: a space that exists because the tension required a physical response and that reproduces the tension within its own operations every month.
