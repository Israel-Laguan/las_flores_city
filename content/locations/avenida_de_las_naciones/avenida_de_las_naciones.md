# Avenida de las Naciones

> Tags: `#transport` `#central-city` `#infrastructure`

## Overview

Avenida de las Naciones was designed as the constitutional city's ceremonial spine — a north-south boulevard that would express, in physical form, Las Flores Free City's aspiration toward internationalism and civic dignity. When it was completed in 2038 alongside the city's formal founding infrastructure, the avenue was lined with the flags of the forty-three nations that had extended diplomatic recognition, and its central median was planted with a sequence of native tree species selected by a horticultural commission as living markers of the city's ecological ambitions.

By 2077 the flags have multiplied to sixty-one, each pole now alternating between national colours and the brand standards of the corporate sponsors who fund the avenue's maintenance contract — a concession arrangement that the city council approved in 2064 after the infrastructure authority's maintenance budget was cut by thirty percent. LW Group holds three of the twelve primary sponsorship positions.

## Notable Places

### Ceremonial Flag Mile
The prestige central stretch between the financial district and the government quarter, lined with sixty-one national flags alternating with corporate sponsor banners. LW Group holds three of the twelve primary sponsorship positions.

### Monorail Elevated Section
A 2069 addition running seven metres above the avenue's central carriageway. Its supporting columns create recessed spaces that serve as semi-permanent encampments for the city's homeless population.

### Corporate Banner Array
Holographic-cloth banner units along the central median, connected to a content scheduling system administered by an LW Group subsidiary.

---

## Conclusion

Avenida de las Naciones is the city's most legible statement of what it aspires to project and what it has actually produced. The flag poles and the sponsorship banners occupy the same poles; the ceremonial median and the monorail columns share the same boulevard. The encampments beneath the elevated structure are not an anomaly but a direct consequence of the infrastructure investment decisions that produced the structure above them.
