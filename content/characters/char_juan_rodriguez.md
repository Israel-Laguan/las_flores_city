# Juan Rodríguez

**Title (full):** Editor-in-Chief & Managing Director, El Informador de Las Flores

**Description (full):**

Juan Rodríguez is a 78-year-old journalist and the legendary Editor-in-Chief of *El Informador de Las Flores*, the last independent newspaper in the city. Born into a family of indigenous activists in the rural outskirts of San Miguel del Monte, he has spent nearly six decades exposing corruption, defending the marginalized, and refusing to let the truth be buried—no matter how many times they've tried to silence him. In an era of corporate media monopolies and AI-generated news, Juan's paper remains a bastion of old-school investigative journalism, printed on recycled paper and distributed by a network of trusted street vendors.

## Background

Juan was born in 1999 in San Miguel del Monte, where his parents were organizers in the *Movimiento por la Tierra y la Dignidad* (Movement for Land and Dignity). His childhood home was a hub for activists, journalists, and displaced families, and he learned early that information was both a weapon and a target. After his father's notes were burned in a police raid when Juan was 12, the family relocated to Las Flores.

He studied journalism at the National University of Las Flores, specializing in community reporting. His early career at the now-defunct *La Voz del Pueblo* was marked by groundbreaking investigations: the 2023 Valle de las Sombras massacre (which tied agribusiness land grabs to police executions), the disappearance of 47 children from a state-run orphanage (linked to a human trafficking ring with police ties), and the poisoning of the Río de las Flores by a textile factory—his first major environmental win, which led to the first corporate conviction under the country's environmental laws.

By 2030, he co-founded *El Informador* as a response to the growing corporate control of media. Over the next 47 years, he broke stories that toppled governors, exposed cartel ties to city hall, and documented the slow erosion of civil liberties in the name of "progress." The paper survived multiple assassination attempts (including a 2045 car bombing that killed his driver), government bans, digital hacking campaigns, and the 2060 media purge that shut down 17 competing publications.

## Personality

Juan is a man of contradictions—cynical about the world but hopeful about people, stubborn to a fault but fiercely protective of his team, a technological Luddite who recognizes the necessity of digital tools. He carries himself with the weary dignity of someone who has seen too much but refuses to look away. His deep-set eyes seem to hold the weight of every story he's ever covered, and his voice, though rough from decades of chain-smoking hand-rolled cigarettes, still commands attention when he speaks.

He is known for his unwavering principles: zero tolerance for bribes, a refusal to accept government or corporate advertising, and a commitment to printing the names of the dead—even when other papers won't. He speaks in measured tones most of the time, but when angered, his words become sharp as a blade. He has a dry, dark sense of humor that often catches people off guard, and he's been known to quote poetry or folk songs at unexpected moments.

His office above the newsroom is cluttered with framed front pages from the paper's biggest scoops, a wall of red string connecting photos of sources and suspects (his analog version of a digital investigation board), and a small shrine to the 17 reporters and staff who have died in the line of duty over the years.

## Motivations

"El periodismo no es un oficio, es un acto de resistencia." ("Journalism isn't a profession; it's an act of resistance.")

Juan's mission is simple: to ensure that the truth survives in a city where it's become a commodity. He believes that a society without independent journalism is a society that has given up on itself. In 2077, as megacorporations tighten their grip on Las Flores and the government uses increasingly sophisticated surveillance to control the narrative, Juan sees *El Informador* as one of the last lines of defense for the city's soul.

He seeks to mentor the next generation of journalists, knowing he won't live forever. His daughter, Camila (now 20), has begun interning at the paper, and though he fears for her safety, he's determined to pass on his knowledge. He also wants to transition the paper to a cooperative ownership model before he retires, ensuring it can't be bought or shut down by corporate interests.

## Alliances

Juan maintains a network of allies across Las Flores' marginalized communities. His closest ties include his sister **Cecilia Rodríguez**, a nurse who runs a clinic in San Miguel del Monte and often tips him off to health crises the government ignores; **Father Mateo**, a liberation theology priest who shelters journalists and sources in his church; and **Lupita "La Chispa,"** a retired gang leader turned community organizer who provides security for reporters in dangerous neighborhoods.

He has a complex relationship with the city's other media outlets. **Oscar Mendoza**, editor of the pro-corporate *El Diario de Las Flores*, is his longtime rival, though they share a grudging mutual respect. The Valdez Cartel has offered Juan "protection money" 17 times over the years; he's refused every time, though the cartels still plant stories in rival papers to discredit *El Informador*.

Juan also collaborates with **Yara Rossi**'s restoration efforts, seeing environmental justice as intrinsically linked to social justice. He's provided investigative support to expose illegal waste dumping in Old Las Flores and the corporation behind the 2072 toxic algae bloom in the bay.

## Approach

Juan is a firm believer in journalism as a public service, not a business. He champions grassroots initiatives that give voice to the voiceless, regularly hosting informational forums in neighborhoods, printing special editions in indigenous languages, and maintaining a whistleblower hotline with encrypted messaging.

His methods are a blend of old and new: he still carries a notebook everywhere and insists reporters take handwritten notes during sensitive interviews (no digital trails), but he's also embraced encrypted communication tools and trained his staff in digital security. The paper uses a mix of print distribution (through trusted vendors) and digital (via encrypted apps and the dark web) to reach audiences.

He's particularly known for his **"La Esquina"** ("The Corner") section, written by and for the city's street vendors, sex workers, and undocumented migrants; his **whistleblower hotline**, which has broken 38 major stories in 47 years; and his **free media literacy workshops**, offered in partnership with local universities.

Despite the dangers, Juan insists on leading by example. He still goes into the field for major stories, though his staff tries (and usually fails) to convince him to stay in the office. "If I'm asking them to risk their lives," he'll say, "I can't ask them to do what I won't."
