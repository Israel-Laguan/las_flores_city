# Xue Lihua

> **Born:** 2000 (Chengdu, Sichuan Province, China)
> **Role:** Former military operative, restaurateur
> **Status:** Active (as of 2059)

## Physical Description
- Hair: Black with silver streaks, worn in a practical bun
- Eyes: Dark brown, sharp and alert from years of covert work
- Build: Slender and strong, moves with military precision
- Skin: Fair with weathered lines from decades of cooking
- Distinguishing features: Small scar on left cheekbone (knife fight in training), simple cotton apron over plain clothes, always alert posture, calloused hands from kitchen work

## Background

### Early Life in China
Born Xue Lihua in 2000 Chengdu, the second daughter of a retired People's Liberation Army logistics officer and a public school teacher. Her childhood was shaped by rigid discipline and scarcity — the tail end of China's economic restructuring left her family in the working class. She enlisted at eighteen, following her father's path, and was selected for signals intelligence training after scoring highly on aptitude exams.

Her handlers noticed a rare combination: technical skill paired with convincing emotional warmth. In 2028, at age twenty-eight, she was recruited into a covert foreign operations unit tasked with establishing long-term embedded assets in South America — specifically in mining regions where Chinese state-owned enterprises held extraction rights.

### The Las Flores Assignment
Xue arrived in Peru under the identity of a young Chinese immigrant seeking work. Her cover: a modest restaurant in Old Las Flores, positioned to attract mine managers, local politicians, and anyone else of interest. The restaurant was her listening post. For the first five years, she sent regular encrypted reports to a handler known only as "Director Zhou."

Then the reports stopped being acknowledged. At first she assumed technical difficulties. Then years passed. By 2038, she understood: her superiors had moved on. The mining concessions had shifted, the geopolitical priorities had changed, and no extraction order would ever come. She was alone in a foreign country with no way home and no one expecting her return.

### Transformation
The abandonment broke something — and rebuilt it. Xue dismantled the dead-drop protocols, burned her remaining cipher materials, and poured everything into the restaurant. What had been a cover became her identity. She learned to cook not as performance but as craft, sourcing ingredients from Chinese immigrants who ran underground supply chains through Lima. Her hand-pulled noodles became famous in the barrio; her mapo tofu drew customers from the Chinese community and beyond.

She never stopped watching. The military habits never left — she noted license plates, remembered which mine workers came and went, tracked which officials accepted her food and which refused. But she stopped reporting. The data accumulated in her memory with nowhere to go, until it simply became part of her awareness rather than intelligence.

## Personality

Stern without cruelty, efficient without coldness. Xue operates on a framework of obligations: she feeds people, and that act of feeding is both kindness and control. She gives freely but always on her terms. Her humor is dry and rarely surfaces, and when it does, it catches people off guard.

She struggles with vulnerability. Decades of cover have made sincerity feel dangerous, so she expresses care through action — an extra portion, a repaired kitchen tool, an errand run without being asked. Her relationship with Kusi is the only space where she allows something unguarded, and even there, her affection is filtered through decades of emotional armor.

## Relationship with Kusi
Among her regular customers was an indigenous Quechua man in his fifties named Kusi, a former miner who had drifted into odd jobs after the mine closures. Their bond was unconventional from the start: she gave him chores, slipped extra money into his pay, and called him her "pet." What began as a power dynamic — she the employer, he the dependent — evolved into genuine companionship. He was the only person who saw through the soldier to the person beneath.

In 2059, at age fifty-nine, Xue received official notice releasing her from military duty — a form letter from a bureaucracy that had long since forgotten her existence. That same day, she asked Kusi to marry her. He said yes without hesitation. Their relationship defies simple labels: a cross-cultural bond forged in loneliness, dependency, and stubborn mutual recognition.

## Secrets

Xue has never told Kusi about her true past. He believes she is simply an immigrant who arrived years ago and stayed. She has also never revealed the scope of intelligence she gathered over decades — some of which, if surfaced, could implicate former mine officials in corruption or worse. She keeps this knowledge locked away, unsure whether it is a burden, a weapon, or simply evidence of a life that never had a purpose.

## The Restaurant
Her restaurant, "Xue's Kitchen," is a narrow space on a side street in Old Las Flores with eight tables, a hand-painted sign, and no menu board. Regulars know what she serves; newcomers must ask. The kitchen is spotless and she allows no one inside it except Kusi, who washes dishes. She closes at three in the afternoon and never opens on Sundays.
