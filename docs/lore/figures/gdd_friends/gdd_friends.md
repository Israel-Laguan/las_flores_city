# Alex's Friends — The 2077 Core Group

> Tags: `#figures` `#2077` `#movement` `#allies` `#las_estrellas`
>
> **Status:** Carlos Lacan — Deceased. Miguel Jhonson, Ana Kim, Isabella Vargas — Active (2077 and beyond).
>
> **Note on surnames:** The core group reflects Las Flores's multicultural heritage. Ana uses the surname Kim (of Asian descent), Miguel Jhonson (of Anglo-Caribbean descent), Carlos Lacan (of European/French-Caribbean descent), and Isabella Vargas (of Latin American descent).
>
> **Individual Profiles:** See [Miguel Jhonson](miguel_jhonson.md), [Carlos Lacan](carlos_lacan.md), [Ana Kim](ana_kim.md), [Isabella Vargas](isabella_vargas.md) for detailed character information.

## Overview

Miguel, Carlos, Ana, and Isabella were not assembled. They accreted around Alex the way people do when someone they care about is doing something dangerous and important: one at a time, for different reasons, with different limits.

None of them were revolutionaries. None of them had a political theory or a grievance history or a prior experience with the kind of thing the Las Estrellas investigation would become. What they had was a connection to Alex—through friendship, through work, through coincidence—and the characteristic human difficulty of watching a person you know doing something alone that they should not be doing alone.

By the time the 2077 movement reached its climax, they had all paid for the involvement. Carlos paid the most. The others carried what they paid forward.

## Miguel Jhonson

**Role:** Logistics, Infrastructure, Operational Trust
**Age (2077):** ~24
**Background:** Born and raised in Las Flores's western district, from a large extended family. Works part-time in a logistics company while completing a degree in urban management.

### Who He Is

Miguel is physically large—broad shoulders, unhurried movement, the kind of presence that makes a room feel slightly more stable simply by entering it. He is not imposing in the way of people who want to be imposing. He is grounding. Calm without being distant, protective without being controlling, funny in the specific way of people who have grown up surrounded by others and learned to read a room before speaking.

He is also, and this matters, intensely practical. While Alex thinks in terms of what should be built, Miguel thinks in terms of what the building requires—the supply line, the schedule, the contingency plan for when the schedule fails. He does not romanticize the work. He does it.

Miguel is a local in a way that Alex never quite becomes, and this distinction runs through everything. He has roots in the city's western district that go back generations; he knows which neighborhoods will trust an organizer and which will ask hard questions first; he knows who to call when a safehouse needs to be relocated at 11 PM. This knowledge is not dramatic. It is load-bearing.

### His Arc

Miguel's involvement in the Las Estrellas investigation begins with skepticism and ends with commitment. He listens to Alex's theory about the HVAC murders with the patience of a good friend who suspects the theory is wrong but does not want to dismiss it without evidence. When he sees the evidence, the skepticism becomes competence: *okay, so what do we need and who do we call?*

Carlos's death hits Miguel differently than it hits Alex—not with less force, but with more direction. He grieves by working. He does not allow himself extended introspection during the crisis, but he carries it, and it shows up later in the exactness of his care for the people around him. He takes fewer risks with other people's lives after Carlos. He is more precise about what he asks of others.

During the movement, Miguel builds the logistical network that makes everything else survivable. Safehouses are found and maintained. Communication protocols are established and rotated. Supply caches—medical supplies, communication hardware, documents—are distributed across the city's western and northern districts through contacts that Miguel has cultivated over years of ordinary life in the neighborhood. When the crackdowns come, they damage the movement's edges. They cannot reach the distributed core that Miguel has made resilient.

### After Alex

Miguel is the one who was there at the end. He has never spoken publicly about those final minutes. He joined the transitional council and spent the years after the broadcast in the unglamorous work of institutional rebuilding—housing policy, infrastructure remediation, community liaison with the international environmental oversight committees. He is still doing it. He does not find it satisfying in the way that the movement was sometimes satisfying. He finds it necessary.

He still lives in the western district. He visits the bench near the renamed complex sometimes, usually early in the morning, before the neighborhood fills up. He does not stay long.

## Carlos Lacan

**Role:** Technical Expert, Emotional Heart
**Age (2077):** ~24 (deceased)
**Background:** Electrical engineering student from a working-class family on the city's northern edge. Won a partial scholarship; covers the remainder through part-time technical work at an electronics repair shop.

### Who He Was

Carlos was the person who said yes before you finished the sentence. Not out of recklessness—he was careful with technical work, methodical, precise about specifications and tolerances—but out of a kind of instinctive loyalty that found decline harder than the thing being declined.

He was also funny. Not in the polished way but in the way of someone who observed things accurately and reported them honestly, which produces humor as a byproduct. He had a habit of narrating what he was doing when he worked—a running technical commentary that was partly concentration and partly performance, a way of making the people around him feel included in what he understood.

He was deeply, unambiguously kind. Not the performed kind, not the kind that requires an audience. The kind that makes people feel like they are not a problem or an inconvenience, that makes them feel actually seen by the person talking to them.

### His Arc

Carlos's entry into the investigation is through Miguel, who knew him from shared coursework. When Miguel explains what Alex has found in the building—the HVAC anomalies, the deaths, the roof apparatus—Carlos's response is to ask what they need him to look at. The technical aspects of the sabotage are, from an electrical engineering perspective, sophisticated but not incomprehensible. He confirms the theory within two roof inspections and immediately begins planning the camera installation.

He was braver than he let on. He volunteered for the riskier tasks—the rooftop work at night, the physical maintenance of the surveillance setup—not because he was indifferent to danger but because he understood that being afraid of something was not the same as being unwilling to do it. He had done this calculation enough times that it had become habitual.

His death in the car chase is the story's central wound. It is the moment that transforms the investigation from an amateur inquiry into a personal reckoning. Alex never stops carrying it. The framing of the operation that follows—every decision about acceptable risk, every moment of restraint with other people's lives—is made in the shadow of what happened to Carlos.

### Legacy

Carlos is remembered by the group as the person who made the technical work feel possible and the danger feel less isolating. He is remembered by the movement, insofar as the movement knew him at all, as the first person who died for what the investigation would become.

He had a younger sister. She was informed of his death before the public broadcast. She does not grant interviews. She keeps a small framed photograph of him at the electronics shop counter where he used to work.

## Ana Kim

**Role:** Early Investigator, Eventual Conscientious Objector
**Age (2077):** ~25
**Background:** Works at the university library where Alex has a part-time shift. Studying social work. Grew up in Las Flores's middle districts with both parents; has a younger brother.

### Who She Is

Ana is organized, direct, and honest in a way that sometimes reads as severity but is actually just accuracy. She says what she thinks, including inconvenient things, without the softening scaffolding that many people deploy around difficult truths. This is not unkindness. It is a form of respect—the decision that the person she is talking to can handle the real information.

She is also, underneath the directness, warmhearted in a way that she does not always advertise. She is the person who notices when someone has been working too long without eating and says so. Who keeps track of who is struggling and finds indirect ways to help. Who, when Ana finally leaves the investigation, does so not out of selfishness but out of a considered, honest evaluation of what she can sustain without destroying herself.

### Her Arc

Ana's entry into the investigation is accidental and then deliberate. She comes to return Alex's forgotten notebook; she ends up helping follow a car. She is in the chase that kills Carlos because she was already there, because she said yes to a small thing and the small thing became large.

After Carlos's death, Ana continues with the investigation. She does the archival work with Isabella, and makes a specific, useful contribution that the others could not have: she uses her university library employee access to retrieve archived press databases—finding the newspaper article that identified four of the building's victims as former Minera Estrella PR staff. It is not glamorous detective work. It is Ana knowing which system to search and having the credentials to search it. This is characteristic of her: practical, competent, applying what she already has.

She is also, through these weeks, the person who brings food to the planning sessions. Who notices that Miguel has not slept. Who asks the question everyone else is sidestepping. She keeps the group functional in ways that have no formal title.

The break comes at the City Hall archives. The physical confrontation with the guards injures her—a swollen eye, bruised ribs, a wrist that would bother her for months. She makes her decision in the car on the way back, while Isabella is already talking about what the documents confirmed. She waits for a full stop, then says: *I'm done. Not because I don't believe this. Because I have to be able to live my life, and this is beyond what I can give it.*

She does not apologize. She thanks Isabella specifically—for pulling her out of the guard's grip during the fight, at the moment when Isabella could have left her to run for the records room. That Isabella did both—freed Ana and still got the documents—is something Ana acknowledges without quite resolving. The gratitude is genuine. The feeling of having been tactically weighed in the moment does not fully disappear.

She leaves. No one holds it against her.

This is, in a different story, the cowardly choice. In this story, it is the honest one. Ana understands what she can sustain. She respects that limit without dressing it up as something nobler. The movement, when Alex builds it, will be full of people who give what they can and stop when they have to, and this is not failure—it is what makes the movement sustainable.

### Her Contribution

Ana never returns to the frontlines. She sends money twice, in amounts that are meaningful against her budget, through channels she constructs carefully. She does not make contact until after the broadcast, when she reaches out to Miguel with a short message: *I heard. I'm glad it worked. I'm glad you're alive.*

She lives a peaceful life. She meant what she said. This is its own kind of commitment: the willingness to value what you have and protect it honestly rather than sacrifice it to something that would consume you.

Her testimony about the investigation's early phase—provided to the international forensic review team—is thorough, accurate, and delivered without drama. She does not frame herself as either hero or coward. She describes what happened and what she did.

## Isabella Vargas

**Role:** Intelligence, Analysis, Pattern Recognition
**Age (2077):** ~23
**Background:** Architecture and urban studies student (same cohort as Alex, different specialization). Her grandfather lives alone in Las Flores's eastern district. A family member—an uncle—died under circumstances similar to the Las Estrellas victims, several years before the investigation, in a case that was closed as an accident. This was never proven, but it was never satisfactorily explained either.

### Who She Is

Isabella is observant in the way that people who have had to be observant are observant—not as a personality quirk but as a survival mechanism refined into skill. She notices what is anomalous. She tracks patterns across data sets that appear unrelated. She is patient with ambiguity in a way that the investigation requires and others find difficult.

She is not easy to read. She does not share emotional states freely, and her exterior calm in high-pressure situations has at various points been mistaken for detachment. She is not detached. She is focused, which is different. The anger she carries about her uncle's death—never confirmed, never resolved—runs underneath everything she does in the investigation with a quiet, consistent heat.

She is also, when comfortable, dry and funny and observant in the way that produces uncomfortable moments of accuracy. She and Alex balanced each other: Alex's warmth brought people in; Isabella's precision kept the work honest.

### Her Arc

Isabella arrives at the café table where the shattered group is sitting after Carlos's death. She has overheard enough of the conversation to understand what is happening, and she has her own reason to be in it. She does not perform the joining; she sits down, introduces her connection to the case, and asks what they need.

She becomes the investigation's intelligence function almost immediately. The spreadsheet she creates—cross-referenced deaths, victims' backgrounds, shared affiliations across the city and three other jurisdictions—is the tool that allows the photograph to be found. She builds the pattern before she can explain it, which is the characteristic experience of working with data that resists obvious structure. The pattern becomes obvious in retrospect. Before that, it is days of iteration and false leads and dead-end connections.

The City Hall archive break-in is Isabella's initiative, and it begins with a specific impatience. The camera surveillance operation Adeyemi had organized felt passive to her—watching a clock, waiting for a killer to appear in frame. She does not wait for things. When passive surveillance stops producing movement, she creates movement.

She takes Ana and Miguel. When the guards find Miguel and Ana in the corridor, Isabella does not stay to help—she splits. She has already calculated that the records room matters more than the corridor. She reaches it, photographs what she needs, and is gone before the guards understand she has been there. On the way out, she knocks out a guard who has Ana pinned, because that guard is between her and the exit and because Ana needs it—but the sequence of that decision, the calculation underneath it, is not something Isabella examines closely or apologizes for.

The documents confirm everything they suspected. The building on contaminated land. The bribes. The approval trail.

In the car afterward, Ana waits for a stop and says she is done. She thanks Isabella specifically—for pulling her out. Isabella says *of course*. There is a version of this where that resolves cleanly. The version that actually happened is slightly off, in a way neither of them names.

After Ana leaves, Isabella becomes the anchor of the intelligence operation. She is the liaison between Alex's visible movement and Evelyn's shadow network—one of the few people who knows both sides of the operation and can translate between them. She is also the person who eventually identifies the paper trail connecting Mayor Vega and Senator Chen to Li Wei's financial network, which becomes the direct target of the estate raid.


### After Alex

Isabella is not a public figure in the way that Miguel or Ana become, even marginally. She continues doing intelligence work in the transitional period—identifying corruption within the new institutional structures, providing information to the international oversight bodies, tracing the financial networks that had supported the LW Group's capture of the city's economy.

She also pursues, through the transitional government's forensic review process, the reopening of the case that closed her uncle's death as an accident. The investigation is ongoing.

She visits the university's urban studies library sometimes—not the one where Alex worked, a different one, quieter. She still has the spreadsheet from the Las Estrellas investigation. She has added columns to it over the years, tracking what happened to each victim's case in the subsequent legal proceedings.

Every box is filled in now. Every case has an outcome. She finds this, in a way she does not fully explain, important.

## The Group Together

What made the group work was the combination of what each person brought and—more importantly—the specific way their limitations balanced. Alex provided direction and heat but needed to be restrained from taking risks that endangered others. Miguel provided stability and logistics but needed to be pushed past his instinct to protect rather than act. Carlos provided precision and courage but was not cautious enough about his own safety. Ana provided the mirror of honest limits that kept the group from consuming itself. Isabella provided the intellectual structure that made the investigation mean something beyond reaction.

Each of them was necessary. None of them was sufficient. This is what made them a group and not simply a collection of useful individuals.

They lost Carlos. They almost lost Ana to the archive scuffle. They lost Alex at the end.

What survived was not the group—that specific configuration of people in a specific crisis does not survive the crisis intact—but the thing they had built together. The movement, the evidence, the network, the distributed infrastructure of trust across Las Flores's neighborhoods. That survived, and the three remaining members built their lives forward through it.
