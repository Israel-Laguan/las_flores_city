# Alex Garcia

> Tags: `#figure` `#protagonist` `#2077` `#movement` `#rebellion` `#architect` `#las_estrellas`
>
> **Born:** ~2055, coastal town south of Las Flores
> **Role:** Architecture Student, Leader of the 2077 Grassroots Movement
> **Status:** Deceased (2077)

---

## Overview

Alex Garcia is the central protagonist of the 2077 endgame arc. Born into the generation that inherited the poisoned city, Alex grew up knowing the cost of corporate greed in the most intimate terms—in the slow deterioration of a mother's health, in the smell of a house where someone is dying but no one names the reason. Unlike Evelyn Ruthenberg, who operates in the shadows and has spent twenty years hardening herself into something cold and purposeful, Alex is irreducibly human: warm, funny, distracted, sometimes cowardly, always searching. That humanity is precisely what makes them dangerous.

Alex does not arrive in Las Flores as a hero. They arrive late to class, anxious about their scholarship, eating lunch alone in a city that feels enormous and indifferent. The transformation from frightened student to the symbol of a city's liberation is not a dramatic awakening. It is a slow, painful accumulation of things witnessed and ignored, of guilt that accumulates until it becomes impossible to carry and must be set down as action.

---

## Physical Appearance

Alex is lean and of medium height, with the kind of frame that reads as younger than they are—an effect reinforced by the worn backpack they always carry, perpetually overstuffed with sketchbooks, dog-eared urban planning texts, and a battered thermos of coffee. Their hair is dark and usually in need of a cut. Their hands are covered in pencil marks and the occasional ink stain. They dress practically—jeans, a hoodie, old sneakers—because practicality is what the scholarship budget allows.

By the time the movement is in full swing, Alex has traded the hoodie for a jacket that has passed through too many hands to have a clear owner anymore. They look older. Not harder, exactly. Just more sure of the weight they carry.

---

## Personality

Alex is the person who hesitates before helping and then never forgives themselves for hesitating. This guilt is not performative; it is the engine of almost everything they do. On the day they moved to Las Flores, they watched a car crash from ten feet away and stood frozen while three strangers—street artists with paint-covered jeans—were already running toward the wreckage. Alex helped eventually. But *eventually* left a mark.

They are genuinely funny in small groups, prone to tangents about architectural history when nervous, and terrible at accepting compliments. They tutor younger students not just because it pays but because explaining things gives them patience for themselves. They have a habit of sketching the buildings around them when they are waiting—on napkins, on the margins of lecture notes, on the backs of receipts. Miguel once found a perfect detailed elevation of the apartment complex done on the back of a grocery list.

Beneath the warmth is a deep streak of stubbornness. Once Alex decides something is wrong, they cannot let it go. They cannot sit with injustice as a background fact of life. This is both their greatest strength and the thing that pulls everyone around them into danger they did not sign up for.

## First Days in Las Flores

### The Car Crash

On one of Alex's first days in the city, rushing to Professor Mendoza's sustainable urban design lecture, they witnessed a crash that would define their relationship with crisis. A self-driving delivery truck and an older model sedan collided in a tangle of mangled metal. The sedan's driver, a middle-aged man, was frantically trying to free himself from the wreckage, then reaching back into the car to pull out a woman.

Alex stood frozen, feet rooted to the pavement. The pragmatic part calculated the minutes ticking away, the distance growing between them and their class. But another part was horrified by the unfolding scene. A crowd had gathered — some filming with detached curiosity, others looking on with vacant eyes, some continuing their day as if nothing had happened.

Just as shame began to surge, three young men — street artists by the look of their paint-splattered clothes — burst through the crowd. One sprinted to the car, calling reassurances. Another began directing traffic. The third assessed the damage with calm precision. Their decisiveness broke the spell, and a wave of helping hands — including Alex's — joined the effort.

Alex helped clear debris and push the mangled car from the main thoroughfare. By the time the ambulance arrived, the couple was out of the car, shaken but alive. Relief washed over Alex, followed by a wave of introspection. They had helped — but only after others had taken the initiative. The question *why* would haunt them for months.

### The City's Architecture

Weaving through crowds on the way to class, Alex couldn't help but geek out at the cityscape. Buildings that scraped the sky, sustainable technology woven into everyday life — solar lamps, self-driving taxis, structures that looked like they were designed by aliens. Every detail, from the bioluminescent trees that lit paths at night to the kinetic pavements generating energy with each footstep, whispered of the future Alex yearned to build.

Las Flores was the most advanced city along South America's Pacific coast, trailing just behind Santiago de Chile in architectural and technological achievement. It represented the future Alex dreamed of shaping — if they could keep their scholarship.

### The Apartment Building

Alex's new apartment building stood in stark contrast to the glittering skyline. It was a squat, concrete block lost in a maze of similar structures, each window a flickering television screen offering glimpses into the lives of its inhabitants. The lobby smelled of stale cooking and disinfectant — a familiar scent that reminded Alex of their childhood home in a small coastal town.

The apartment itself was a cramped studio with peeling paint and a view of a brick wall, a far cry from the sleek, futuristic dwellings Alex dreamt of designing. Yet amidst the chipped tiles and flickering fluorescent light, Alex found a strange sense of comfort. It was their own little corner in this sprawling metropolis.

One evening, a burst of salsa music erupted from the apartment next door, vibrating through the thin walls. Alex felt a flicker of annoyance at first, then a reluctant smile. It was a sound of life, of connection, of the human spirit refusing to be subdued by the anonymity of the city. Leaning out the window, Alex watched the cable cars gliding across the night sky like fireflies tracing patterns against the darkness. For a moment, they felt a sense of belonging.

But the feeling didn't last. The rhythmic salsa faded into murmurs from the patio below. An elderly woman lamented the discovery of a deceased neighbor, found alone in his apartment days after his passing. A younger woman chimed in — another recent solitude death, a middle-aged woman just five floors down. Two deaths, so close, both alone. It felt like a morbid coincidence, a grim reminder of the city's isolating underbelly.

The old woman's words echoed: *"Living alone... no one to see them..."* It was a reality Alex had chosen, sacrificing connection for ambition. The price suddenly felt steeper, the potential isolation at the end of their journey a chilling prospect.

## Background & Early Life

Alex was born in a small coastal town south of Las Flores to a family of modest means. Their father worked as a municipal maintenance technician; their mother, Rosa García, cleaned offices in the commercial district of the nearest city. The household was not poor by the standards of the region, but it was precarious—the kind of precarious where a single broken appliance or a hospital visit reshuffled everything.

Rosa developed respiratory problems in the early 2060s. She was a woman who cooked with her kitchen window open, who sang along with the radio during the evening news, who remembered the names of every one of Alex's schoolteachers. The cough came first, then the fatigue, then the diagnosis of a kind the local clinic could not afford to treat properly. No one said the words *lithium contamination* in those years. The connection would not become public until much later. What Alex knew was that their mother was getting worse, and then worse, and then gone.

Rosa died when Alex was sixteen. The official cause listed a respiratory complication. The real cause—the lithium-saturated groundwater that had been seeping into downstream communities for a decade, the direct legacy of Minera Estrella's 2052 catastrophe—would not be confirmed until the 2077 data broadcast. Alex would not live to read the report bearing their mother's community as a documented harm site. But they died knowing.

After Rosa's death, Alex's father withdrew into silence and long work shifts. Alex channeled everything into school. They were brilliant in a way that their teachers found slightly exhausting—not because of arrogance, but because of the relentless follow-up questions, the unwillingness to let a concept remain fuzzy, the habit of connecting things that were taught as separate subjects. A guidance counselor mentioned the **Van der Meer Foundation** scholarship. Alex applied three times before being accepted.

## The Scholarship & University Life

In 2075, Alex enrolled at the **Universidad Nacional de Las Flores** to study sustainable urban design. The scholarship covered tuition and a modest living stipend. It did not cover the gap between the stipend and the actual cost of living in a city where rents had climbed steadily for twenty years, so Alex supplemented income with two part-time jobs: tutoring first-year mathematics and working evening shifts at the university library.

The city was everything Alex had imagined and more disorienting than expected. Las Flores moved at a speed and scale that had no equivalent in the coastal town. The bioluminescent trees along the promenade, the kinetic pavements that generated electricity from foot traffic, the elevated rail threading through the skyline like a silver needle—Alex found it all beautiful and slightly alienating. They were an outsider in a city of people who had grown up inside it, and the difference felt permanent in ways that were hard to articulate.

Their closest friend was **Miguel**, a local student from a large family in the western district. Miguel was physically imposing, slow to anger, and possessed of an inexhaustible patience for other people's moods. He did not share Alex's obsession with urban design, but he liked listening to Alex explain it, and he was the first person in Las Flores to make Alex feel like they actually belonged somewhere.

---

## The Move to Residencial Las Estrellas

In early 2077, Alex's previous housing arrangement—sharing a flat with two other scholarship students—collapsed when one of them dropped out and the other moved in with a partner. Facing the housing market with a budget that left little room, Alex found **Residencial Las Estrellas**, a concrete-and-glass apartment complex near the train station in the Sector Norte. The rent was low for a reason: the building was old, the neighbors were older, and the city around it had been quietly deprioritized for investment.

Alex's unit was a cramped studio on the fourth floor with a view of a brick wall and a temperamental boiler that knocked at three in the morning. They painted one wall dark blue. They propped their sketchbooks on the windowsill. They told Miguel it was fine.

The neighbors were mostly retired, most of them solitary. Alex learned their rhythms quickly—the building imposed a kind of intimacy. Doña Herminia on the third floor who made noise with the TV after midnight. The old gentleman on the sixth who kept impeccable hours and never took the elevator. The two widows on the seventh who shared a balcony and fed pigeons with a consistency that made Alex feel there was still some order in the world.

They would all be dead within months.

## The Investigation

### First Deaths: The Pattern Forms

Alex noticed the absence before they registered the deaths. One evening the television from Doña Herminia's apartment was silent, and the silence felt wrong. Three days later, a neighbor mentioned in passing that the old man on the sixth had been found in his apartment—no one quite knew when he had died, only that the smell had eventually reached the hallway.

Five days after that, a woman on the fifth floor died in her sleep. The paramedics cited carbon monoxide poisoning from a malfunctioning air conditioning unit. The building management apologized and offered to pay for a technician. The police came, asked questions, and left.

Alex lay awake that night and could not explain the unease. Studied architecture teaches you to read buildings—load-bearing points, structural logic, the way systems interact. Something about the deaths and the building's HVAC layout didn't sit right. They said nothing. They tried to sleep.

A week later, late at night, they heard footsteps above them where the roof access was. They went up. A figure, moving quickly in the dark. By the time Alex reached the roof, the figure was gone. Left behind on the concrete was a utility knife—the kind used for electrical work.

### Adeyemi Enters

**Chief Inspector Adeyemi** was not what Alex expected of a senior detective. He was tall, methodical, and entirely too perceptive. He arrived at Alex's apartment and listened to the roof story with the kind of focused patience that could pass for either deep interest or barely-concealed irritation. He asked why Alex had gone up to investigate alone.

Alex said they weren't sure. Which was true.

Adeyemi studied them for a moment and then said, carefully, that they were the only one who had seen anything, and that they should call him immediately if they saw anything else. He left. Alex felt as if they had narrowly avoided something without knowing what.

They went back to the roof the next day and found a concealed electrical apparatus—a device rigged to the building's HVAC control lines. Someone was operating the air conditioning remotely, targeting specific units. Three days later there was another death.

Alex called Adeyemi. By the time the inspector arrived, the apparatus was gone.

### The Friends

Alex went to Miguel. They expected skepticism. They got it, and then they watched the moment Miguel looked at the photographs Alex had taken—blurry, phone-camera shots of the apparatus before it disappeared—and went quiet and serious in the way he did when something had moved from theory to fact.

Miguel knew **Carlos**, an electrical engineering student from their faculty. Carlos was the kind of person who said yes before the favor was fully explained, who showed up twenty minutes early, who made difficult technical things sound like puzzles worth solving. He confirmed within two visits to the roof that the HVAC system could absolutely be hijacked to introduce carbon monoxide into targeted units by disabling ventilation while feeding back incorrect temperature readings.

They installed a camera on the roof. They set up a watch rotation. Alex started a private folder of notes. They felt, for the first time, like they were doing something instead of waiting.

**Ana** entered by accident. She was a coworker from Alex's library shift, sharp and warm and consistently more competent than the role required. Alex had forgotten a notebook in their locker; she came to the café where they had agreed to meet, and Alex arrived distracted, looking past her, noticing a car in the street that matched the description of the vehicle they had captured on camera.

Alex asked Ana to help follow it. This is one of the things Alex never quite forgave themselves for—the casualness of that request, as if what was happening was a favor, not a risk. Ana said yes.

**Isabella** joined at the café table where the four of them sat afterward, still shaken from the chase. She had overheard enough, had been tracking the deaths in the news out of instinct and something more personal (a family member lost to a similar crime years before), and brought to the group a systematic intelligence that none of them possessed. She had spreadsheets. She had cross-references. She had a mind that identified patterns the way other people see familiar faces.

### Carlos

The car chase happened too fast and ended wrong.

Alex had seen the vehicle—an old gasoline car, conspicuous in a city that had largely converted—and there was no deliberate plan. There was a moment where action felt more bearable than inaction. They called Carlos and Miguel. Everyone converged. The car fled. They followed.

On the road to the city's outskirts, a motorcycle appeared from a side street. It didn't swerve or brake. It cut directly across Carlos's lane. His car went off the road.

Alex and Miguel pulled over. Alex reached Carlos first.

He was already gone.

The motorcycle was not a random accident. Alex knew it then and could not prove it and could not make themselves not know it. Carlos had gone to the roof because Alex had asked him. Carlos had confirmed the HVAC sabotage because Alex had needed him to. Carlos had been in that car because Alex had made a call.

The grief was total. What followed it—a cold, specific fury—was something Alex had never felt before. It didn't crowd out the grief. It organized it.

### The Conspiracy Unfolds

Isabella had been working in parallel. The victims' backgrounds, cross-referenced over weeks, yielded a photograph: the 2058 inauguration of a lithium filtration facility near the Río de las Flores. City environmental officers, Minera Estrella executives, all smiling. The caption named the project partners. The victims were all in the photo.

Ana, before she made her choice to leave, found the building ownership records: Residencial Las Estrellas had been co-purchased by a cluster of retired officials and executives who had been in that same photograph. They had taken the land at below-market value from Minera Estrella—land that had never been properly remediated after the mining operations. They had bribed city inspectors to approve construction.

Isabella and Miguel broke into the City Hall records room at night to confirm it. They found the documents. Ana was injured in the fight with the guards and made her choice afterward: *she was done*. She did not apologize. She said she wanted a peaceful life, that she was glad Isabella had saved her, and she left. Alex did not blame her. They had dragged her into this. They had dragged everyone into this.

Adeyemi eventually arrested a former Luz del Río employee—a man whose family had died from the toxic dumping, a man with a credible, tragic motive. He charged him with all the murders. The case was declared solved.

Within two days, two more building owners were dead of heart complications.

Alex and Isabella sat in Alex's apartment for a long time after that. The two remaining owners of the complex—the last surviving witnesses—were still alive. Adeyemi either did not know or did not want to know. The real killer was still operating. The framed man was innocent.

Alex said: *We find the last survivor. We catch her ourselves.*

It would take until then for the word *her* to crystallize in the air. Alex hadn't known. But they were about to find out.

## The Revelation: Meeting Evelyn

The last surviving owner of the complex was a retired city comptroller named Aurelio Sandoval. He had gone into hiding in a rented room on the south side of the city, near the port. Alex and Isabella found him through property records and three days of careful tracing. He was frightened and confused about why he deserved this more than the others—because he had only done what everyone else had done, he said. He had signed what they told him to sign.

They staked out the room in shifts. On the third night, Alex saw a figure in the courtyard below.

What followed was not the violent confrontation Alex had imagined through all the weeks of investigation. They cornered Evelyn Ruthenberg in a stairwell. She was older than the outline Alex had built in their mind. She did not run or fight. She looked at Alex with an exhaustion so complete it was almost peaceful, and she waited.

Alex said: *You killed people.*

Evelyn said: *Yes.*

Alex said: *You killed people who were going to testify. People who could have—*

Evelyn said: *Sit down. I'll show you what they testified about.*

She had documents. Physical copies, worn at the folds from years of careful storage—the internal Minera Estrella emails that confirmed management knew about the structural failures six months before the 2052 disaster. The bribery records. The trial transcripts with the exculpatory testimony mysteriously absent. The list of victims from downstream communities.

And at the end, quietly, Evelyn said: *What town are you from?*

Alex told her. Evelyn opened a different folder and handed them a page. It was a health survey conducted by an NGO in 2068, documenting chronic respiratory and neurological symptoms in coastal communities downstream from the original contamination site. Alex's hometown was in the first column.

They do not talk much, afterward, about what happened in that stairwell. Isabella, who waited outside, says it took forty minutes. She says when Alex came out, they looked like someone who had just been told a thing they had already known for years.

## The Alliance & The Movement

Alex understood immediately what Evelyn had not been able to see from twenty years of solitary war: that every official Evelyn killed was replaced by another official equally willing to be corrupt. That the LW Group's power was not in its personnel but in the structure of control—the captured media, the bought judiciary, the normalized apathy of citizens who had been taught that nothing ever changed. Assassination was surgery on a system that required revolution.

Evelyn, who had spent two decades relying only on herself, did not easily accept a partner. She accepted a collaborator, then a colleague, then—gradually—something she had no word for. She provided intelligence, tactical support, access to her network of shadow contacts, and the unredacted target list from the USB drive. Alex provided everything Evelyn had lost: the ability to stand in a room and make people feel like their anger was not futile.

The grassroots movement began in Sector Norte, in the neighborhoods closest to the original contamination sites. Alex organized quietly at first—community meetings in back rooms of cafés, translated pamphlets about the Luz del Río verdict and its aftermath, public records requests that exposed the city's environmental failures. They were patient. They were methodical. They knew they were being watched.

When the first public protest happened—three hundred people in a park on a Tuesday morning—the corporate media called it a minor disruption. By the third week, when coordinated strikes shut down two distribution centers and a chemical processing plant connected to LW Group subsidiaries, the Mayor's office used a different word: *threat*.

Miguel ran logistics. Isabella mapped the corporate security networks and found the holes. Ana—who had left, who had chosen peace—sent money through intermediaries. She never came back to the frontlines. Alex never asked her to.

---

## The Crackdowns & The Cost

Mayor Vega unleashed Chief Inspector Adeyemi to suppress the movement. Adeyemi was ruthlessly effective. Safehouses were raided. Liaisons were arrested. The movement's communication networks were compromised twice. Alex spent three weeks sleeping in different locations every night.

The movement did not collapse. It metastasized.

Every crackdown produced more people who had nothing left to lose by joining. Alex understood this not as strategy but as physics: pressure applied to something already compressed does not flatten it. It forces it through whatever crack is available.

Alex found, during this period, that they were good at this. At speaking without notes to a crowd of two hundred people who had never met them and leaving the room with the feeling that something real had just happened. At sitting with a grieving parent and saying things that were true instead of things that were comforting. At making the argument that the city they could build—the actual, physical city, the infrastructure and the housing and the green corridors—was worth the danger of building it. They were an architect. They had always thought in terms of what could be, where others saw only what was.

---

## The Raid on the Mayor's Estate

The final operation was Evelyn's design and Alex's agreement to it. The evidence they needed—the direct link between Mayor Vega, Senator Chen, and Li Wei's financial network—existed in physical and digital form in the estate's secure archive. Getting to it required a coordinated breach of the most fortified private property in Las Flores.

Alex's role was to lead the primary assault team while Evelyn infiltrated the archive. Their job was to create enough controlled chaos at the estate's perimeter that Evelyn could move through the interior undetected.

They both understood that the math of the operation did not work cleanly. There were too many corporate enforcers. The timeline for the broadcast required holding the perimeter longer than could be guaranteed.

Alex and Evelyn stood outside the estate in the dark before the breach and Alex said: *Is this the part where you tell me it will be fine?*

Evelyn said: *No.*

Alex laughed. It was a real laugh, the kind that surprised them.

They went in.

---

## The Sacrifice

The broadcast completed at 3:28 AM. The perimeter had been held. But the plan had already gone wrong.

The controlled dam release that was meant to create an escape route had collapsed the dam entirely. The water was surging toward the city — it would leave Las Flores without electricity and threaten thousands of innocent lives. Alex saw the news feed in the command vehicle. They understood immediately what the math meant.

There was a nearby mine, abandoned for decades. If Alex could reach it and detonate the remaining explosives, the resulting cave-in could create an improvised dam — redirecting the flood, saving the city, and buying Evelyn and the others the cover they needed to escape.

Alex did not tell anyone what they were doing. They drove to the mine alone.

Miguel figured it out too late. He reached the mine entrance just as the ground shook. He does not talk about what he found.

Alex died in the explosion, buried under a mountain of rock and rubble, having traded their life for the city they had spent their final months trying to save.

## Legacy

The broadcast aired globally. The LW Group's concealed ownership of Las Flores's political infrastructure, the covered-up scope of the Lithium Leak's casualties, the names and documented evidence against fifty-three city officials, judges, corporate officers, and one sitting senator—it was all there. The response was immediate and impossible to suppress.

Li Wei's arrest came four weeks later in Santiago de Chile, where he had fled. Mayor Vega was killed by Adeyemi inside his own hidden safe room — a secret Adeyemi carried to his grave as part of the carefully staged narrative that Evelyn Ruthenberg had killed the mayor. Chief Inspector Adeyemi, who had turned on the mayor after Evelyn's plea reached him, emerged from the estate with Evelyn in handcuffs for the world's cameras. He coordinated the initial wave of arrests, his own past complicity publicly known and publicly debated, spending the rest of his career in that ambiguity.

Evelyn's staged capture led to two trials. The terrorism trial of Miguel, Isabella, and Ana collapsed under international scrutiny after Adeyemi's carefully worded testimony. Evelyn's own trial for the mayor's murder became the defining legal event of the transition — she testified for three days, laying out the complete history of the Great Lithium Leak cover-up. She was convicted on reduced charges of manslaughter, sentenced to fifteen years, and served four before her sentence was commuted.

Alex's name became the shorthand for the movement. Not because of strategy or martyrdom or symbolism—those things came after—but because of something simpler. Alex had been a regular person in a bad situation who had been unable to stop caring about it. People recognized that. It was recognizable to everyone who had ever stood frozen at the edge of someone else's crisis and then, eventually, stepped in.

Miguel, Isabella, and Ana—who came forward publicly in the weeks after the broadcast—guided the first transitional council. They are the ones who did the long, unglamorous work of stabilizing the city. Alex had built the fire. They kept it from burning everything down.

The architectural school at Universidad Nacional renamed one of its studios. It was a small thing. The plaque just says: *For someone who understood that cities are built by people, for people.*
