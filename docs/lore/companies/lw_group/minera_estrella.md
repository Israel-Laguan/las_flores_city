# Minera Estrella

> Tags: `#lw_group` `#lithium` `#mining` `#environmental` `#trial` `#cover_up`

## Overview

**Type:** Mining Joint Venture
**Partners:** China Lithium & Metals + Local Government of Las Flores
**Parent Affiliation:** LW Group
**Primary Output:** Lithium and other minerals
**Notable Incident:** The Great Lithium Leak (August 15, 2052)
**Status:** Acquitted of major charges (2057), operating under "improved" safety standards

Minera Estrella is a joint venture between the Chinese conglomerate China Lithium & Metals (backed by LW Group) and the local government of Las Flores. Established during a period of minimal regulatory oversight, the company has significantly influenced both the economic and political landscapes of Las Flores — most controversially through the catastrophic 2052 lithium leak and the subsequent cover-up.

---

## The Great Lithium Leak (2052)

### The Disaster
On August 15, 2052, a high-pressure pipe carrying lithium hydroxide exploded at Minera Estrella's container facility, 10km from the Andean Mine. The accident released massive quantities of lithium hydroxide into an affluent of the Río de las Flores, causing:
- Hundreds of deaths from toxic exposure in downstream communities
- Catastrophic ecosystem damage — the river turned from vibrant blue to sickly yellow-green
- Long-term contamination of soil, groundwater, and agriculture
- Nicknamed "The Great Lithium Leak" by the media

### Internal Warnings Ignored
Months before the disaster, internal reports highlighted structural weaknesses in the containment systems. **Qiu Bo** proposed a new containment system, but **Zhang Liang** imposed financial barriers that delayed approval. An independent audit in 2049 confirmed critical vulnerabilities, yet no action was taken.

### The Cover-Up
After the disaster, Minera Estrella's leadership orchestrated an elaborate cover-up:
- **Zheng Wuhao** (Facility Supervisor) downplayed the severity in initial reports
- The leak was falsely attributed to an "electrical failure" caused by a storm
- Evidence was fabricated, including a controlled explosion to simulate electrical grid failure
- Technicians were bribed to produce false reports
- Internal safety reports were suppressed
- Whistleblowers were intimidated or disappeared

## Trial and Acquittal (2055–2057)

### Charges
The Special Environmental Tribunal tried Minera Estrella on:
- **Gross Negligence**: Ignored safety protocols leading to catastrophic failure
- **Environmental Damage**: Unprecedented pollution of ecosystems and water supplies
- **Public Endangerment**: Thousands exposed to health risks
- **Bribery and Corruption**: Bribing officials to overlook violations

### Key Evidence
- Internal documents proving awareness of structural weaknesses (Liu Fang's evidence)
- Financial records showing payments to government officials
- Environmental impact assessments confirming contamination scale

### Defense Strategy
The defense — led by **Xiao Chen** (future CEO of LW Group) — argued the disaster was unforeseeable, blaming heavy rainfall and an unprecedented electrical failure. They exploited:
- Statute of limitations on bribery charges
- Jurisdictional challenges
- Evidence admissibility disputes
- Corporate liability shielding (executives not personally liable)

### Verdict (January 10, 2057)
Minera Estrella was **acquitted** on most charges. The company paid a $5 million fine and promised improved safety standards. The verdict was widely criticized as corporate impunity.

### Post-Trial
- Minera Estrella sued **Luz del Rio** (a small electric company), blaming it for the electrical failure narrative
- Luz del Rio was driven to bankruptcy by December 2057
- "Safe Waters Act" passed — regional legislation requiring stricter environmental protections (largely symbolic)

## Operations

### The Andean Mine
- Open-pit and underground lithium mine
- Raw materials transported 10km to the container facility via heavy-duty trucks
- 24/7 operations with ~150 personnel at the facility

### Processing Stages
1. **Roasting**: Minerals roasted to convert to lithium carbonate (Li2CO3)
2. **Refining**: Carbonate refined into lithium hydroxide (LiOH) using high-pressure pipes
3. **Shipping**: Processed lithium shipped via Jade Dragon Ports or Great Dragon Energy rail

### Workforce
- Pre-leak: ~150 personnel at facility, indirectly supported thousands more
- Post-modernization (2055+): Heavy automation reduced workforce; early retirement/transfer offers extended

---

## Modernization & Workforce Reduction

Post-leakage, Minera Estrella embarked on extensive modernization:
- Enhanced infrastructure with AI-driven processes
- Significant workforce reduction
- Early retirement or transfer policy to other LW Group companies
- "Improved" safety protocols — criticized as superficial by environmental groups
