# Political System of the Free City of Las Flores

> Tags: `#governance` `#politics` `#government` `#free_city` `#elections`

## Overview

The political system of Las Flores is structured around a Governor, District Mayors, City Council, and an independent judiciary. The system is designed to balance executive, legislative, and judicial power while ensuring representation across the city's diverse districts.

## Governor

The Governor is chosen directly by the people in an election. The term "Governor" is used instead of "Mayor" to distinguish the leadership of the Free City given its special status. The Governor's job is to run the city day-to-day, suggest new laws, and make important decisions that affect everyone in Las Flores. The Governor is the face of the city and leads it for a set number of years before there's another election.

## Mayors (District Mayors)

In Las Flores, each district has its own "Mayor." While the overall city is led by the Governor, each district's Mayor is like a mini-Governor for that specific area. They're responsible for managing the needs of their district, like fixing roads or improving parks, and they work with the District Councils to make decisions for their parts of the city.

## Political Parties

Just like in any democratic system, Las Flores has political parties. These are groups of people who have similar ideas about how the city should be run. They choose candidates to run for different positions in the city government, like the Governor, the City Council members, or the District Mayors, in elections. People in the city can join a party, support a party, or just vote for candidates they like in the elections. The parties compete with each other to get their candidates elected, trying to convince voters that their ideas and plans are the best.

## Summary

The Governor leads the whole city, the Mayors look after their specific districts, and the political parties help get people elected to these positions. This system helps spread out the responsibility of running the city and ensures that different areas and groups of people have a say in how things work.

## Terms of Office

**Governor, Mayors, and City Council Members:**
- Each of these officials serves a term of 4 years.
- They can be re-elected for one more term, making a total of 8 years in office if they are re-elected.
- Currently, we have one city council representative for every 10,000 residents.

## Judges

- Judges in district courts can serve for up to 35 years.
- Judges on the Supreme Court have terms of 5 years.
- To become a Supreme Court judge, one must be elected by fellow judges and have more than 5 years of experience in the judiciary.
- Judges can be removed temporarily or permanently if they commit crimes, with the Supreme Court handling cases where lower internal courts fail to reach a clear decision.

## Transition of Power

When a new official takes office (like a new Governor or Mayor), they can only replace up to half of the current staff. This rule is designed to protect experienced and competent public servants and ensure continuity in government services.

This system aims to strike a balance between allowing fresh leadership and maintaining experienced government workers, ensuring stability and ongoing expertise in the city's administration.
